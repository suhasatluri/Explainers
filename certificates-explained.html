A certificate is just a passport for a machine: it says who you are, and it's signed by an authority that others already trust.

Every confusing word on the list — leaf, intermediate, root, chain, keystore, truststore — is one small part of border control. We'll walk through the whole checkpoint, one stamp at a time.

Before Any Passport — The Signature

The one thing only you can do

A passport is worthless if anyone could forge your signature. So identity online starts with a pair of keys: a private key you never, ever hand over, and a public key you print for the world. Anything signed by the private key can be checked by the public key — but never reproduced from it.

🔒 Private key

Stays locked in your pocket. Used to sign. Lose control of this and someone can impersonate you.
••••••••••••••••••••••••••

🔓 Public key

Printed in your passport for anyone. Used to verify a signature — never to create one.
3A:9F:C2:1D:7E:B4:08:…
— signature appears here —

Why this matters for everything below: the private key is the root of your identity. A certificate doesn't contain it — a certificate is your public key plus your name, wrapped up and signed by an authority.

So when people say "install the certificate," there are really two halves: the public certificate (shareable) and the private key (secret). Mixing those up is the first place things go wrong.

At the terminal

A keypair and a self-signed pair in one line: openssl req -x509 -newkey rsa:2048 -keyout key.pem -out cert.pem -days 90. The key.pem is the secret half; cert.pem is the shareable half.

Who Vouches For Whom

Root, Intermediate, Leaf

Authority comes in tiers, exactly like a government. The top almost never signs anything directly — it delegates. Tap each tier to meet it.

Root certificate
The national authority · in the vault
▲ signs ▲
Intermediate certificate
The regional passport office
▲ signs ▲
Leaf certificate
Your actual passport · the end-entity

Why three tiers? So the most precious key — the root's — can stay offline in a vault and sign almost nothing. Day-to-day passports are issued by intermediates. If an intermediate is ever compromised, you revoke just that office, not the whole country.

In the real world — the #1 production bug

"Missing intermediate" is the classic. The server must send its intermediate(s) alongside the leaf, because clients usually only ship roots. Forget it and some clients still work (browsers cache intermediates or fetch them via the cert's AIA URL) while others — curl, Java, older clients — fail with unable to get local issuer certificate. The infamous "works in my browser, breaks in prod." Diagnose with openssl s_client -connect host:443 -showcerts to see exactly what the server sends.

Getting Your Passport

Applying for a certificate (the CSR)

You don't print your own passport — you apply. The application is called a Certificate Signing Request, and the golden rule is right there in the steps: your private key never leaves your hands.

1

Make your keys

Generate a private + public key pair on your own server. The private half stays here, forever.

private key — keep
2

Fill the application

Bundle your public key with your identity (your domain). That bundle is the CSR — a passport application with your photo attached.

CSR — send to CA
3

Authority verifies

The CA checks you really control that domain, then signs your public key with its key — an unforgeable seal of approval.

proof of control
4

Receive the passport

Back comes your signed certificate (the leaf), plus the intermediate to present with it. Install both with your private key.

certificate — issued
At the terminal

Create a request: openssl req -new -newkey rsa:2048 -keyout key.pem -out request.csr. You hand over request.csr only. Notice the private key.pem is never uploaded — that's the entire security model in one detail.

The Great Confusion, Settled

Keystore vs Truststore

Almost everyone who's lost an afternoon to certificates lost it right here. Two boxes, two completely different jobs. Once you see them as your wallet vs the guard's recognition list, the confusion evaporates.

🎒

Keystore

Who am I? What do I present?

Your wallet. Holds your own private key and your own certificate (with its chain). This is the identity you show others. It's secret — password-protected.

📋

Truststore

Whom do I believe?

The guard's recognition list. Holds the root (CA) certificates you're willing to trust. No secrets here — just the authorities whose signatures you'll accept.

SORT EACH ITEM INTO THE RIGHT BOX

🎒 Keystore — who I am

📋 Truststore — who I trust

That's the whole rule. Your secrets and your own identity go in the keystore. The authorities you're willing to believe go in the truststore. A web server mostly needs a keystore (to present its cert); a client mostly needs a truststore (to verify the server).

And when a client also carries a keystore — so it can present its own certificate back — that's mutual TLS: both sides show passports.

In the real world — Java, files, and formats

Java exposes these as javax.net.ssl.keyStore (your identity) and javax.net.ssl.trustStore (whom you trust); the default truststore is the JDK's cacerts file of bundled roots. Formats: .p12/.pfx (PKCS#12, the modern cross-platform bundle of cert + key + chain), .jks (legacy Java-only — Java now defaults to PKCS#12), .pem (Base64 text), .key (private key), .crt (a certificate). Inspect with keytool -list -keystore store.p12.

Putting It Together

The crossing, end to end

Every HTTPS connection runs this checkpoint in milliseconds. Here's the whole sequence in plain terms.

01
Traveller approaches. Your browser opens a connection to the server and says hello.
02
Passport presented. The server sends its leaf certificate — and the intermediate(s) with it.
03
Guard follows the chain. Browser checks leaf←intermediate←root, validity dates, and that the name on the passport matches the site it asked for.
04
Anchor in the trust store. The chain ends at a root the browser already trusts. If not — the scary warning page.
05
Admitted. A shared secret is agreed using the keys, and everything after is encrypted. The padlock appears.

The Last Stamp

If you remember one thing: a certificate is a passport, and the chain of trust is just following who vouches for whom until you reach an authority you already trust.

  • A certificate = your public key + your identity, signed by an authority. The private key stays secret and separate.
  • Leaf is your passport, intermediate is the issuing office, root is the top authority — kept offline.
  • The chain of trust climbs leaf → intermediate → root; verification only succeeds if it ends at a trusted root.
  • Keystore = who I am (my key + cert). Truststore = who I trust (the roots). Don't mix them.
  • Servers present a keystore; clients verify with a truststore; when both present, it's mutual TLS.
  • Most failures: expired, name mismatch, self-signed, untrusted root, or a missing intermediate.
